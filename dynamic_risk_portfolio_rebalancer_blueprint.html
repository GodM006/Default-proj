01 Executive Summary

Enterprise lending portfolios carry a structural blind spot: risk assessment is concentrated at origination and rarely updated continuously thereafter. Once a loan is approved, the borrower's risk profile is typically monitored only through lagging indicators — missed payments, delinquency flags — by which point loss mitigation is already expensive or impossible.

The Dynamic Risk Portfolio Rebalancer (DRPR) addresses this gap. It is not a credit scoring system. It is a risk migration and drift monitoring platform that continuously computes the divergence between a loan's original underwriting risk estimate and its current inferred risk state, using repayment progression signals derived entirely from the LendingClub portfolio dataset.

02 Business Problem

Traditional banking credit infrastructure operates as a one-shot pipeline: an application is received, underwriting assigns a risk grade, and the loan is approved or denied. Beyond approval, monitoring is reactive — triggered by delinquency events rather than by early detection of deteriorating borrower health.

The Gap

  • A borrower graded B at origination (historical default rate: 13.39%) may have meaningfully deteriorated to what would be a D-equivalent risk level (30.38%) within 18 months.
  • Without continuous reassessment, the portfolio's true risk-weighted exposure is systematically understated.
  • Risk officers cannot distinguish between a loan that is performing according to underwriting expectations versus one that is drifting toward default silently.
  • Provisioning, capital allocation, and intervention triggers all rely on stale origination-era risk grades.

What DRPR Does Not Do

DRPR does not replace underwriting. It does not score new applications. It operates entirely post-origination on an existing book of loans, monitoring the divergence between the underwritten PD and the current estimated PD.

03 Solution Overview

DRPR is a four-layer analytical architecture built on top of the LendingClub loan dataset. Each layer contributes a distinct signal to the final dynamic risk assessment.

Layer 1
Underwriting Anchor
grade, sub_grade → origination PD
Layer 2
Repayment Progression
Payment ratios, months since last payment
Layer 3
Vintage Risk
issue_d cohort macro adjustments
Layer 4
Latent State HMM
Hidden borrower states: Healthy → Default

These four layers converge in the Dynamic PD Engine, which computes PD(t) — a time-varying probability of default that is then compared against the origination PD to produce the Risk Drift Score. That score drives the Intervention Engine, which recommends risk-tiered actions.

06 Underwriting Anchor Framework

The underwriting anchor is the origination-era risk estimate that DRPR uses as a baseline against which current risk is measured. LendingClub assigns each loan a grade (A–G) and a finer sub_grade (A1–G5), which represent the platform's assessment of borrower credit risk at origination.

Empirical historical default rates by grade are treated as the ground truth origination PD:

GradeOrigination PDRisk LevelDefault Bar

Sub-grades (e.g., B1 through B5) allow finer interpolation within each grade band. The sub_grade therefore provides a more precise origination PD anchor than grade alone. This 35-point sub_grade scale becomes the starting risk coordinate for every loan in the portfolio.

The underwriting anchor is fixed at origination. It does not change. It is the reference point against which all subsequent risk drift is measured. The anchor is only as good as the original underwriting — a critical limitation discussed in Section 18.

Origination PD Construction

For the MVP, origination PD is constructed as a lookup table mapping sub_grade → empirical default rate, interpolated linearly within each grade band. A logistic regression trained on origination-era features (grade, sub_grade, fico_range_low, fico_range_high, annual_inc, int_rate, dti, emp_length) serves as a secondary anchor to validate and refine these lookup values.

07 Repayment Progression Framework

Repayment progression is the primary dynamic signal available in the LendingClub dataset. As a loan ages, the cumulative repayment trajectory encodes meaningful information about the borrower's ability and willingness to service debt — the two core drivers of credit risk.

Four engineered metrics form the repayment signal vector:

Repayment Ratio
total_rec_prncp / loan_amnt
Measures what fraction of the original principal has been recovered. Low values relative to loan age indicate underpayment or payment shortfalls — a strong early-warning signal of stress.
Interest Recovery Ratio
total_rec_int / total_pymnt
Measures the proportion of total payments composed of interest rather than principal. Higher-than-expected interest share may indicate the borrower is paying minimum amounts only, reducing principal reduction rate and extending effective duration risk.
Payment Efficiency
total_rec_prncp / total_pymnt
The complement of interest recovery. High efficiency means principal is being retired aggressively, implying a borrower performing ahead of schedule. Low efficiency signals concern about debt service capacity.
Months Since Last Payment
months_between(last_pymnt_d, reference_date)
Payment recency is a powerful leading indicator. A borrower who made a payment 1 month ago has materially different risk from one whose last payment was 5 months ago, even if cumulative amounts are similar. This metric bridges repayment history to the latent state model.
Months Active (loan age)
months_between(issue_d, reference_date)
Normalizes all repayment metrics by loan age. A repayment ratio of 0.4 on a 12-month-old 36-month loan is on-track; the same ratio on a 30-month-old loan signals underpayment. All metrics must be interpreted in the context of expected amortization schedule.

Expected vs Actual Repayment

Each loan has a scheduled amortization profile computable from loan_amnt, int_rate, and term. The deviation between expected cumulative principal at month t and actual total_rec_prncp is the repayment deviation signal — arguably the most direct proxy for borrower financial health available without transaction-level data.

08 Vintage Risk Framework

Not all loans of the same grade carry identical risk. A Grade B loan originated in 2014 — before the 2015–2016 LendingClub credit standard tightening — may have systematically different default characteristics than a Grade B loan from 2017, even if the borrower profiles appear similar. This is vintage risk: the macroeconomic and underwriting-standard environment embedded in a loan cohort at origination.

Vintage Identification

Vintages are defined using issue_d, grouped by year-quarter (e.g., 2014-Q1, 2015-Q3). Each vintage cohort accumulates a realized default rate as loans mature and resolve. This realized default rate becomes a vintage-specific adjustment factor applied to the origination PD.

Vintage Adjustment Mechanics

Vintage Default Rate
vintage_PD = (charged_off_in_cohort) / (total_loans_in_cohort)
Computed per issue_d cohort. As a cohort matures, this rate converges toward the true lifetime default rate of that vintage. Earlier (more mature) cohorts provide more reliable estimates.
Vintage Adjustment Factor
vintage_adj = vintage_PD / expected_PD_for_grade_mix
Normalizes the vintage PD by the grade-mix-weighted expected PD. A value >1 indicates the vintage underperformed expectations (adverse macro environment); <1 indicates outperformance.

Macro conditions that embed into vintages include: interest rate environments, unemployment cycles, housing market cycles, and platform-specific underwriting policy shifts. The 2014 and 2015 vintages, for instance, contain loans originated under looser credit standards than 2016–2017, resulting in systematically higher realized default rates controlling for grade.

Vintage risk is a portfolio-level correction. Individual loan PDs are multiplied by the vintage adjustment factor to account for cohort-level macroeconomic effects that were not fully captured at origination. This prevents systematic mispricing of risk in high-risk vintage books.
09 Latent State Modeling Framework

Borrower financial condition does not change instantaneously — it evolves through a sequence of states. A borrower typically transitions from a healthy condition through a watchlist phase, then into stress, and potentially to critical or default over a period of months. These transitions are not directly observed in the dataset. They must be inferred from the observable repayment signals.

This is the foundational motivation for a latent state model: a layer of hidden variables that bridge observable payment metrics and future default risk.

Defined Latent States

Five discrete hidden states are defined. These states do not exist in the dataset — they are an abstraction layer constructed by the model:

Healthy
Repayment on or ahead of schedule. High payment efficiency. Recent payment activity. Low risk drift. No intervention required.
PD(t) ≈ Orig PD
Watchlist
Slight underpayment vs schedule. Payment efficiency declining. Monitoring flagged. Soft outreach recommended.
Drift 0–10%
Stress
Meaningful deviation from repayment schedule. Months since last payment increasing. Proactive intervention required.
Drift 10–25%
Critical
Severe underpayment. High payment gap. Collections escalation triggered. High PD(t).
Drift >25%
Default
Charged Off event observed. Ground truth default. Feeds model retraining pipeline.
loan_status = CO

Hidden Markov Model (HMM) Architecture

A Hidden Markov Model with 5 hidden states is trained on the observable emission sequence: {repayment_ratio, payment_efficiency, months_since_last_payment, repayment_deviation}. The HMM learns:

  • Transition probabilities P(state t+1 | state t): the probability of moving between states in a given period
  • Emission probabilities P(observations | state): the distribution of observable signals given each hidden state
  • Initial state probabilities: influenced by underwriting grade and sub_grade

The Viterbi algorithm is used to decode the most probable state sequence for each loan. Forward-backward inference provides the posterior state probability distribution at any point in time, which feeds directly into PD(t) computation.

HMM implementation uses hmmlearn (GaussianHMM for continuous emissions). The model is trained per-grade segment to account for the fact that healthy Gaussian state emissions differ materially between a Grade A and Grade E borrower.
10 Dynamic PD Engine

The Dynamic PD Engine synthesizes all upstream layers into a single time-varying probability of default, PD(t), computed at any observation point during the loan lifetime.

Origination PD

Origination PD is fixed at loan issuance. It is derived from the grade and sub_grade lookup table of empirical historical default rates, optionally refined by a static logistic regression trained on origination-era features. This is the anchor value — it does not change.

Current PD(t) Computation

PD(t) is computed as a weighted combination:

PD(t) — Dynamic Probability of Default
PD(t) = f(Orig_PD, Repayment_Vector, Latent_State, Vintage_Adj)
In practice: the XGBoost / LightGBM Phase 2 model takes the full feature vector — origination PD, repayment ratio, payment efficiency, months since last payment, HMM posterior state probabilities, vintage adjustment factor — and outputs PD(t) as a calibrated probability.

Model Phases

Phase 1 — Static PD Model: Logistic regression, XGBoost, and LightGBM trained on origination features only (grade, sub_grade, fico, annual_inc, int_rate, purpose, term) with target = default. This establishes the origination PD baseline and benchmarks the dynamic model's incremental value.

Phase 2 — Latent State Integration: The HMM posterior state probabilities (P(Healthy), P(Watchlist), P(Stress), P(Critical)) are added as features. The dynamic model is trained on the full feature set including repayment progression metrics. This produces PD(t).

Phase 3 — Vintage Calibration: PD(t) is multiplied by the vintage adjustment factor to produce the final calibrated current PD.

11 Risk Drift Framework

The Risk Drift Score is the central output of DRPR. It is the signed difference between the current PD and the origination PD:

Risk Drift Score
Risk Drift = PD(t) − Origination PD
Positive drift = borrower has deteriorated relative to underwriting expectations. Negative drift = borrower has improved (favorable early repayment). Magnitude encodes severity; sign encodes direction.

Worked Example

Borrower: Grade B, 36-month loan, issued 2015-Q2
Origination PD (Grade B historical) 13.39%
Repayment Ratio at month 24 (expected: 0.65, actual: 0.31) Below schedule
Months since last payment 4 months
HMM latent state P(Critical) = 0.72
Current PD(t) 38.22%
Risk Drift: Collections Escalation Triggered +24.83%

Why Risk Drift Is Useful for Risk Officers

  • Grade-relative framing: A PD of 25% means different things for a Grade A loan versus a Grade E loan. Risk Drift normalizes by the origination expectation, making cross-portfolio comparison meaningful.
  • Early warning signal: Drift begins accumulating before a missed payment event. The repayment progression and latent state transitions provide leading rather than lagging indicators.
  • Portfolio heat maps: Risk officers can visualize entire loan books as drift distributions, identifying segments, vintages, or origination cohorts experiencing systematic deterioration.
  • Provisioning support: Loans with high positive drift require higher expected credit loss provisions. DRPR provides the quantitative basis for dynamic provisioning adjustments.
  • Regulatory evidence: Under IFRS 9 and CECL frameworks, banks must demonstrate that their provisioning reflects current and forecast credit conditions. Risk Drift scores provide auditable, model-driven evidence of this assessment.
12 Explainability Layer

Any risk model deployed in an enterprise banking context must be explainable — to risk officers, to model validation teams, to regulators, and to internal audit. Black-box PD outputs without attribution are not acceptable in regulated financial services. DRPR uses SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) throughout.

SHAP Application in DRPR

  • Feature-level attribution: For each loan, SHAP values decompose PD(t) into the individual contribution of each input feature. A risk officer can see: "PD increased by 12% because months_since_last_payment increased by 3, and repayment_ratio fell below the grade-B expectation trajectory."
  • Drift attribution: SHAP difference values between PD at t₁ and PD at t₂ identify which features drove the change in risk between two observation points.
  • Portfolio-level aggregation: Mean absolute SHAP values across the portfolio identify the dominant risk drivers at any point in time — useful for macro risk reporting.
  • Watchlist explainability: Every borrower placed on the watchlist receives a top-3 SHAP driver explanation, visible on the risk officer dashboard.

Regulatory Compliance Alignment

SHAP explainability supports compliance with: SR 11-7 (OCC/Federal Reserve model risk management guidance), IFRS 9 significant increase in credit risk (SICR) evidence requirements, CECL forward-looking loss estimation documentation, and GDPR/FCRA adverse action notice requirements (where applicable to consumer portfolios).

Model governance requirement: All models (Phase 1 static, Phase 2 dynamic, HMM latent state) must be registered in the model inventory, assigned model owners, and subject to annual independent validation. SHAP outputs form part of the model validation evidence package.
13 Intervention Engine

The Intervention Engine translates latent state and risk drift scores into tiered action recommendations. It operates as a rule-based system applied over the dynamic risk outputs.

Healthy
No Action. Loan performing within underwriting expectations. Drift ≤ 0. Continue standard monitoring cadence.
Drift ≤ 0%
Watchlist
Soft Reminder. Automated email / SMS reminder about upcoming payment. No human escalation. Log to watchlist queue. Review next cycle.
Drift 0–10%
Stress
Proactive Intervention. Relationship manager outreach. Hardship plan assessment. Potential restructuring options presented. SHAP report attached to case file.
Drift 10–25%
Critical
Collections Escalation. Transfer to collections queue. Legal review initiated. Collateral assessment (if secured). Provisioning flag raised to finance team.
Drift >25%

How Recommendations Are Generated

The intervention recommendation is a deterministic function of: (1) current latent state (HMM posterior argmax), (2) risk drift score, and (3) delta drift — the rate of change of drift over the past 3 observation periods. A borrower drifting rapidly from Watchlist toward Stress triggers an accelerated escalation path even before crossing the Stress threshold.

Key Feedback Considerations

  • Intervention effect confounding: Borrowers who received interventions are not a random sample. The feedback loop must account for selection bias — borrowers who were contacted may have different outcomes than similar borrowers who were not, independent of the intervention itself.
  • Data vintage lag: Outcome data (whether an intervention prevented default) may lag 6–12 months. Retraining must account for incomplete follow-up periods in recent cohorts.
  • Champion/Challenger framework: New model versions are deployed as challengers against the production champion, with A/B assignment and statistical monitoring before promotion.

17 Future Expansion
FUTURE WORK ONLY. The following capabilities are not part of the MVP. They are described here to illustrate how the DRPR architecture could be extended once real-time data pipelines and external data sources become available. None of these should be conflated with the LendingClub-based MVP described above.
Real-time transaction streams (Open Banking / Account Aggregator): With access to checking account transaction streams (salary deposits, utility payments, discretionary spending), the repayment progression layer could be supplemented with direct income and expenditure signals. Sudden disappearance of salary deposits, spike in minimum balance periods, or increased overdraft frequency would become leading indicators of borrower stress — months before repayment behavior degrades.
Behavioral telemetry: Login frequency, mobile app engagement patterns, and digital banking behavioral signals correlate with financial stress in borrower populations. Integration of behavioral telemetry via a customer data platform (CDP) could enrich the HMM emission model significantly.
Alternative data sources: Bureau refresh data (credit score updates), property registry data for HELOC portfolios, employment verification via government databases, and social mobility indices could all improve vintage risk adjustment accuracy and latent state inference.
Real-time Kafka streaming architecture: In an enterprise deployment with continuous transaction data, DRPR would consume a Kafka topic of financial events, maintain a stateful feature store (Feast or Tecton), and serve PD(t) with sub-second latency via a model serving cluster. The HMM transition probabilities would be updated incrementally via online learning.
18 Risks and Limitations
  • Anchor dependency: DRPR's risk drift framework is only as meaningful as the origination underwriting quality. If the origination PD anchor is systematically miscalibrated (e.g., grade inflation in a particular vintage), drift scores will be distorted. This is a known limitation of all anchor-relative monitoring systems.
  • Survivorship bias: The LendingClub dataset contains only loans that were originated. Rejected applications are not visible, creating selection bias in any model trained on this data. The feature distributions of originated loans are systematically different from the full applicant population.
  • LendingClub platform specificity: LendingClub's underwriting methodology, borrower base, and origination standards are specific to the US peer-to-peer consumer lending market. Direct transfer of model coefficients to a bank's mortgage, auto, or commercial lending portfolio would require significant recalibration.
  • No transaction-level data: The MVP cannot observe actual cash flows, income continuity, or spending behavior. All borrower health inference is based on cumulative repayment aggregates, which are lagging indicators relative to transaction-level data.
  • HMM stationarity assumption: The Hidden Markov Model assumes stationary transition probabilities. In practice, transition dynamics change during economic recessions, interest rate cycles, and regulatory shifts. Periodic re-estimation is required.
  • Intervention causality: Risk drift does not identify the cause of borrower distress — only its magnitude. Intervention design should not assume that contacting a borrower will necessarily address the underlying stress, which may be job loss, medical expense, or macroeconomic deterioration outside the borrower's control.
  • Regulatory risk (fair lending): Any model that uses addr_state, purpose, or correlated features could introduce disparate impact on protected classes. Rigorous fair lending testing (adverse impact ratio analysis, ECOA/Regulation B compliance) is mandatory before enterprise deployment.
